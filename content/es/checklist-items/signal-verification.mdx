Signal encrypts your messages so only you and the other person can read them. But in theory, a sophisticated attacker could position themselves in between you and your contact, intercepting messages before passing them along. This is called a "man-in-the-middle" attack. **Safety numbers** let you confirm that your encrypted connection goes directly to your contact with no one sitting in the middle.

**Most people don't need to do this.** If you're chatting with friends about everyday organizing, Signal's encryption is already doing its job. But if you're communicating about something high-risk (direct actions, sensitive legal matters, jouranlist talking with a source), verifying safety numbers is worth the extra step.

Think of it like a wax seal on an envelope. Encryption is the seal, but a sophisticated attacker could break it, read the contents, and reseal it with their own wax. Verifying safety numbers is how you confirm no one swapped the seal.

<HowTo title="How to verify safety numbers">
  1. **Open the conversation** with the person you want to verify > **Tap their name** at the top > **View Safety Number**.
  2. **Compare numbers with your contact:**
     * **In person (best):** Tap "Scan Code" and scan their QR code. If it matches, you'll see a success message.
     * **Not in person:** Read the numbers to each other over a call or video chat where you're confident you're talking to the right person. Don't verify over Signal itself.
  3. **Mark as verified:** If the numbers match, tap the checkmark.

  You only need to do this once per contact unless their safety numbers change (for example, if they get a new phone). If that happens, Signal will notify you. Verify again before sharing anything sensitive.
</HowTo>
